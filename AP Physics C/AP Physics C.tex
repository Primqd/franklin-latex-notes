\section{Kinematics}

\subsection{Scalars and Vectors}
Scalars are numbers. Vectors are vectors. Represent as $\vec{a} = \hat{\imath} + \hat{\jmath} + \hat{k}$.

Dot product of two vectors is the length of one of the vectors projected onto the other multiplied by the length of said other vector. Theta = min angle between two vectors.

\[\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} = |\vec{a}| |\vec{b}| \cos{\theta}\]

Note the following:

\subsection{Displacement, Velocity, and Acceleration}
\[
\vec{v} = \odv{\vec{s}}{t}, a = \odv{\vec{v}}{t} = \odv[2]{\vec{s}}{t} \implies \Delta \vec{s} = \int \vec{v}(t)dt, \Delta \vec{v} = \int \vec{a}(t)dt
\]

Derivatives and intergrals of vector-valued functions can be done component wise.
\[
\vec{s} = x(t)\hat{\imath} + y(t)\hat{\jmath} + z(t)\hat{k} \implies \odv{\vec{s}}{t} = x'(t)\hat{\imath} + y'(t)\hat{\jmath} + z'(t)\hat{k}
\]
\[
\vec{s} = x(t)\hat{\imath} + y(t)\hat{\jmath} + z(t)\hat{k} \implies \int_{t_i}^{t_f}\vec{s}dt = \int_{t_i}^{t_f}x(t)dt + \int_{t_i}^{t_f}y(t)dt + \int_{t_i}^{t_f}z(t)dt
\]

For constant one-dimensional acceleration, kinematic equations apply:
\[\Delta v = \int adt \implies v_f = v_i + at\]
\[\Delta s = \int v(t)dt = \int (v_i + at)dt \implies \Delta s = v_it + \frac{1}{2}at^2\]
\[v_f = v_i + at \implies v_f^2 = v_i^2 + 2v_iat + a^2t^2 \implies v_f^2 = v_i^2 + a(2v_it + at^2) \implies v_f^2 = v_i^2 + 2a\Delta x\]

Falling object experience constant acceleration: $a = g \approx 9.81\unit{\metre \per \second \squared} \approx 10\unit{\metre \per \second \squared}$.
Projectiles experience constant vertical accleration of gravity, but no horizontal acceleration.

\subsection{Reference Frames and Relative Motion}
Let $\vec{v}_{AB}$ = velocity of $A$ relative to $B$.
\[\vec{v}_{AB} = -\vec{v}_{BA}\]
\[\vec{v}_{AC} = \vec{v}_{AB} + \vec{v}_{BC}\]

These are also known as \textbf{Galilean Transformations.} Note these equations will break down near the speed of light due to relativity shit.

\begin{example}
    The president's airplane flies at $250 \unit{\metre \per \second}$ to the east with respect to the air.
    The air is moving at $35 \unit{\metre \per \second}$ to the north with respect to the ground.
    Find the velocity of the president's airplane with respect to the ground.
\end{example}
\begin{answer}
    Let $\hat{\imath}$ = north and $\hat{\jmath}$ = east.
    \[\vec{v}_{PG} = \vec{v}_{PA} + \vec{v}_{AG} = (250\hat{\jmath} + 35 \hat{\imath})\]
    \[|\vec{v}_{PG}| = \sqrt{250^2 + 35^2} \approx 252.4\unit{\metre \per \second}\]
    \[\theta_{PG} = \arctan{\frac{35}{250}} \approx 7.97\unit{\degree} \textrm{ relative to East}\]
\end{answer}

\section{ Force and Translational Dynamics}

\subsection{Systems and Center of Mass}
Recall that the location of a system's center of mass along a given axis can be calculated as follows:
\[\vec{x}_{cm} = \frac{\sum m_i \vec{x_i}}{\sum m_i}\]

This equation can be extended for non-uniform solids by considering differential masses $\odif{m}$:
\[\vec{r}_{cm} = \frac{\int \vec{r} \odif{m}}{\int \odif{m}}\]

One way to calculate $\odif{m}$ is through \textbf{linear mass density}:
\[\lambda = \odv{m}{l}\]

Area and volume mass density exists as well, represented by $\sigma$ and $\rho$ respectively.

\begin{example}
    Suppose you have a bar of length $L$ which has mass density function $\lambda(r) = cr^2$.
    Find the center of mass in terms of $L$, $c$, and fundamental constants.
\end{example}
\begin{answer}
    \[\odv{m}{r} = cr^2 \implies \odif{m} = cr^2\odif{r}\]
    \begin{align*}
        r_{cm} &= \frac{\int r \odif{m}}{\int \odif{m}} \\
        &= \frac{\int_{0}^{L} cr^3 \odif{r}}{\int_{0}^{L} cr^2 \odif{r}} \\
        &= \frac{\frac{r^4}{4} \big|_{0}^{L}}{\frac{r^3}{3} \big|_{0}^{L}} \\
        &= \frac{3}{4} \frac{L^4}{L^3} \\
        &= \boxed{\frac{3}{4} L}
    \end{align*}
\end{answer}

Also recall that for force diagrams, systems can be modeled as a single point located at the system's center of mass.

\subsection{Gravitational Force}

\begin{definition}[Newton's law of universal gravitation]
    \[|\vec{F}_g| = G \frac{m_1m_2}{r^2}\]
    The gravitational force is always exerted along the line connecting the center of masses of two systems.
\end{definition}

This force suggests that a mass sets up a \textbf{gravitational field} around itself: $|\vec{g}| = G\frac{M}{r^2}$.
The word \textbf{weight} is only used when one of the objects is much more massive than the other (usually the Earth).
\textbf{Apparent weight} is the normal force acting on an object, and is what is measured by a scale in an elevator.
Near the surface of the earth, $g \approx 10 \unit{\newton \per \kilogram}$.

\textbf{Newton's Shell Theorem} states \textit{the net gravitational force exerted on an object inside a thin spherical hell is zero} AND \textit{the net gravitational force exerted on an object by a spherical shell is equiavlent to treating the shell as a point mass.}

In practice, the first half of Newton's Shell Theorem suggests that \textbf{within a uniform sphere, only the mass of a sphere within a radius equal to the object's distance from the center} affects it.
All outer layers can be considered as differential spherical shells, and by Newton's Shell Theorem exert no gravitational force.
Thus,
\[m_{partial} = \rho \frac{4}{3} \pi (r_{partial})^3 \textrm{ (this equation is NOT on the sheet)}\]

Using the second half of Newton's Shell theorem and the law of gravitation:
\begin{align*}
    F_g &= \frac{Gm}{r_{partial}^2} * \rho \frac{4}{3} \pi (r_{partial})^3 \\
    &= \frac{4}{3} Gm \rho \pi r_{partial} \\
    &\implies F_g \propto r_{partial} \textrm{ (linear proportionality within the sphere!)}
\end{align*}

\begin{example}
    Find the weight of a 1.0 kg mass located half way to Earth's center. Ignore density variation.
\end{example}
\begin{answer}
    \[F_g \propto r_{partial} \implies F_g = \frac{mg}{2} = \frac{1.0 * 10}{2} = \boxed{5 \unit{\newton}}\]
\end{answer}

\subsection{Kinetic and Static Friction}

Frictional force is exerted to oppose a change in motion on a rough surface, and is proportional to the magnitude of the normal force.
The proportionality constant depends on whether it is moving (\textbf{kinetic}) or stationary (\textbf{static}).
Kinetic friction is usually less than static friction.

\subsection{Resistive Forces}

A resitive force is defined as a \textbf{velocity-dependent} force that opposes an object's velocity.
\[\vec{F}_{res}  = -k\vec{v}\]

Setting up a second law equation with a resistive force will lead to a differential equation for velocity (recall $a = \odv{v}{t}$), which can be solved to lead to an exponential equation for velocity.
Terminal velocity is defined as the maximum velocity reached under a resistive force.

\begin{example}
    A skydiver of mass $m$ drops from rest, and experiences air resistance $F_{air} = -bv$
    Assuming they do not hit the ground, find an equation for their velocity as a function of time.
    State what this suggests about their terminal velocity.
\end{example}
\begin{answer}
    \begin{align*}
        F_{net} &= mg - F_{air} \\
        m \odv{v}{t} &= mg - bv \\
        \frac{m}{mg - bv} \odif{v} &= 1 \odif{t} \\
        \int \frac{m}{mg - bv} \odif{v} &= \int \odif{t} \\
        -m \ln{mg - bv} &= t \\
        mg - bv &= e^{-\frac{t}{m}} \\
        &\implies \boxed{v = \frac{mg}{b} (1 - e^{-\frac{t}{m}})} \\
    \end{align*}
    As $t \rightarrow \infty$, $\boxed{v \rightarrow \frac{mg}{b}}$
\end{answer}

\subsection{Circular Motion}
In order to maintain a cirular path of radius $r$, a particle needs to experience $a_c = \frac{v^2}{r}$ directed towards the center of the motion.
This is known as \textbf{centripetal acceleration}.
The acceleration along the path of travel is known as \textbf{tangential acceleration}.
Any acceleration vector can be split into centripetal and tangential components.

Note that for a vertical, circular loop, an object requires a minimum speed to maintain circular motion as the gravitational force is the only force that causes centripetal acceleration.
\[v = \sqrt{gr}\]

\section{Work and Power}

\subsection{Translational Kinetic Energy}
\[\textrm{KE} = \frac{1}{2} mv^2\]
Note different observers may observe different translational kinetic energies depending on if the observer is moving as well.

\subsection{Work}
\textbf{Work} is the amount of energy transferred in or out of a system by a force exerted by that system over a distance.
Potential energies are always associated with only \textbf{conservative} (path-independent) forces.
The most common \textit{non-conservative} forces are air resistance and friction.

The non-calculus work formula $W = Fd \cos \theta$ can be extended to any path:

\[W = \int_{a}^{b} \vec{F}(r) \cdot \odif{\vec{r}}\]

This formula suggests that ony the component of the force parallel to displacement can change a system's energy, and suggests that work is equal to the area under the curve of a graph of $F_{\parallel}(r)$.

The \textbf{work-energy theorem} states the change in an object's kinetic energy is equal to the sum of the work being done by all forces. 

\subsection{Potential Energy}

The relationship between conervative forces exerted on a system and the system's potential energy is

\[\Delta U = - \int_{a}^{b} \vec{F}_{cf}(r) \cdot \odif{\vec{r}}\]

($\vec{F}_{cf} = $ conservative forces) Or, alternatively (not on the equation sheet)

\[\Delta U = -W_{cf} \textrm{ (potential energy is converted into work)}\]

This definition can be used to prove the potential energy equations for springs and universal gravitation:

\begin{proof}[Proof of spring energy]
    \begin{align*}
        \Delta U_{spr} &= -\int \vec{F}_{spr} \cdot \odif{\vec{r}} \\
        &= - \int -kx \odif{x} \\
        &\implies \boxed{\Delta U_{spr} = \frac{1}{2} kx^2}
    \end{align*}
\end{proof}
\begin{proof}[Proof of universital gravitational energy]
    \begin{align*}
        \Delta U_{g} &= -\int \vec{F}_g \cdot \odif{\vec{r}} \\
        &= - \int G \frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2} \odif{r} \\
        &\implies \boxed{\Delta U_g = G\frac{m_1 m_2}{r}}
    \end{align*}
\end{proof}

The intergral definition of potential energy suggests that

\[F_{cf} = -\odv{U}{x}\]

This further suggests \textbf{stable equlibria} occur at local minima of the potential energy function ($F_{cf}$ opposes change) while \textbf{unstable equilibria} occur at the maxima of the potential energy function ($F_{cf}$ supports change)

\subsection{Conservation of Energy}
\[\textrm{ME} = \textrm{KE} + \textrm{PE}\]
\[\Delta \textrm{ME} = W_{nc} = \textrm{work transferred out of the system}\]

\subsection{Power}
\[P_{avg} = \frac{\Delta E_{transferred}}{\Delta t} = \frac{W}{\Delta t} \implies P_{inst} = \odv{W}{t}\]

If an object is moving at constant velocity $\vec{v}$ due to an external force $\vec{F}$, then the power transferred to the object is
\[P = \vec{F} \cdot \vec{v}\] 
This usually applies to the force that a car exterts to drive at a given velocity.

\section{Linear Momentum}

\subsection{Linear Momentum}
\[\vec{p} = m \vec{v}\]
\subsection{Change in Momentum and Impulse}
\[\vec{F}_{net} = m\vec{a} = m \odv{\vec{v}}{t} = \odv{\vec{p}}{t} \]
Impulse is defined as follows:
\[\vec{J} = \int_{t_i}^{t_f} \vec{F}_{net}(t) \odif{t}\]

\begin{definition}[Impulse-Momentum Theorem]
    The impulse exerted on an object is equal to the object's change in momentum.
    \[\vec{J} = \Delta \vec{p}\]
    \begin{proof}
        This is a direct result from Newton's Second Law:
        \[\int_{t_i}^{t_f} \vec{F}_{net} \odif{t} = \int_{t_i}^{t_f} m \odv{\vec{v}}{t} \odif{t} = \int_{t_i}^{t_f} m \odif{\vec{v}} = m\vec{v_f} - m\vec{v_i} = \Delta \vec{p}\]
    \end{proof}
\end{definition}
\subsection{Conservation of Linear Momentum}
\[\vec{v}_{cm} = \frac{\sum \vec{p}_i}{\sum m_i}\]
\[\vec{p}_{sys} = \sum \vec{p}_i\]

From the impulse-momentum theorem, in the absence of external forces (and thus impulse) the momentum of a system does not change.

From Newton's Third Law, the impulse exerted by one object on a second object is equal and opposite to the impulse exerted by the second object on the first.

\subsection{Elastic and Inelastic Collisions}

\begin{definition}[Elastic Collision]
    collision in which initial KE = final KE.
\end{definition}
\begin{definition}[Inelastic Collision]
    Collision in which initial KE is not equal to final KE (lost to $W_{nc}$).
    \textbf{Perfectly inelastic} when objects stick together and move with same velocity after collision.
    This results in the maximal loss of energy. 
\end{definition}

\section{Torque and Rotational Dyanmics}

\subsection{Rotational Kinematics}
\textbf{Counterclockwise motion} is considered a \textbf{positive} change in angle.

Like linear motion, there exists \textbf{angular velocity} $\omega = \odv{\theta}{t}$ and \textbf{angular acceleration} $\alpha = \odv{\omega}{t} = \odv[2]{\theta}{t}$.
These definition lead to analgous \textbf{rotational kinematic equations} for constant rotational acceleration:
\[\omega_f = \omega_i + \alpha t\]
\[\Delta \theta = \omega_i t + \frac{1}{2} \alpha t^2\]
\[\omega_f^2 = \omega_i^2 + 2 \alpha \Delta \theta\]

Note that angular velocity and acceleration are "pseudovectors" that point in the direction of the axis of rotation.
\textit{The right-hand grip rule can be used to determine the direction of these pseudovectors, but this is not needed for the exam.}

\subsection{Connecting Linear and Rotational Motion}
Recall the definition of arc length:
\[\Delta s = r \Delta \theta\]

This definition of arc length can be used to show the following:
\[v_T = r \omega\]
\[a_T = r \alpha\]
Note for both of these equations, they result in the \textbf{tangential component} of velocity and acceleration respectively.

\subsection{Torque}
Recall torque results from the perpendicular component of force relative to the position vector from the axis of rotation to the point of the force.
This is mathematically represented as a cross product:
\[\vec{\tau} = \vec{r} \times \vec{F}\]
Like angular velocity and acceleration, the direction of the torque is a pseudovector, the sign indicating whether it rotates counterclockwise (positive) or clockwise (negative).
Recall that torque is the rotational analog to forces.

\begin{example}
    Suppose you have a bar of length $L$ which has mass density function $m(r) = cr^2$.
    If the bar is fixed at $r = 0$, find an equation for the magnitude of the torque on the bar when the bar is $\theta$ below the horizontal.
\end{example}
\begin{answer}
    Recall from Example 2.1 that $r_{cm} = \frac{3}{4} L$.
    We can consider the gravitational force as acting on the center of mass.
    \[m = \int_{0}^{L} cr^2 \odif{r} = \frac{cL^3}{3}\]
    \begin{align*}
        |\vec{\tau}| &= |\vec{r}||\vec{F}_g|\sin(90 - \theta) \\
        &= \frac{3}{4} L * \frac{cL^3}{3} * g \cos{\theta} \\
        &= \boxed{\frac{cgL^4}{4} \cos{\theta}}
    \end{align*}
\end{answer}

\subsection{Rotational Inertia}
The \textbf{rotational inertia} of an object represents its resistance to changes in rotation, and is analgous to mass in linear motion.
The rotational inertia of an object of mass $m$ rotating at a distance $r$ away from the axis of rotation is as follows:
\[I = mr^2\]
Because the rotational inertia of multiple objects sum, we can express it as an intergral:
\[I = \int r^2 \odif{m}\]

\section{Electric Charges, Fields, and Gauss's Law}
\subsection{Electric Charge and the Electric Force}
Charge... exists. It's scalar and measured in SI units \textbf{Coulombs}.

\textbf{Coulomb's Law} describes the electrostatic force between two charged particles:

\[|\vec{F}_E| = \frac{1}{4\pi \epsilon_0} \frac{|q_1q_2|}{r^2} = k\frac{|q_1q_2|}{r^2}\]

$\epsilon_0$ represents the \textbf{electric permittivity of free space} and has a constant value (units $\unit{C^2 N^{-1} m^{-2}}$).
Electric permittivity measures "the degree to which a material or medium is polarized in the presence of an electric field" and is determined by "the ease with which electrons can change configurations within a given material."
Electric polarization can be modeled as the induced rearangement of electrons by an external electric field.

\subsection{Conservation of Electric Charge and the Process of Charging}

Like charges repel. Charges on system influence each other.


\subsection{Electric Fields}

Electric fields exist. $\vec{E} = \frac{\vec{F_e}}{q_e} = k \frac{q}{r^2}\hat{r}$

Note while in electrostatic equilibrium (no net movement of charges), the excess charge of a conductor is distributed on the surface of said conductor, and the electric field within said conductor is 0.
Note that this is \textbf{not} true for insulators: charge is distributed through the surface AND interior, and the electric field within may be non-zero.

\subsection{Electric Fields of Charge Distributions}
For continious charge distributions, you can consider tiny point charges $\odif{q}$ and apply Coulomb's Law to each of them, then add their effects together.
The fact that you can add them together is known as \textbf{the principle of superposition.} Mathematically:

\[\vec{E} = k \int \frac{\odif{q}}{r^2} \hat{r}\]

You can typically directly solve for Q or express the integral in terms of $\odif{r}$ using \textbf{linear charge density} $\lambda = \odv{Q}{L} \approx \frac{Q}{L}$, \textbf{surface charge density} $\sigma = \frac{Q}{A}$, or \textbf{volumetric charge density} $\rho = \frac{Q}{V}$

\begin{example}
    Determine the electric field at point P.
    \begin{center}
        \includegraphics[scale=0.5]{ex_lin_e.png}
    \end{center}
\end{example}
\begin{answer}
    \[\lambda \odif{r} = \odif{Q}\]
    \begin{align*}
        \vec{E} &= k \int \frac{\odif{Q}}{r^2} \hat{r} \\
        &= k \hat{r} \int_{a}^{a+L} \frac{\lambda \odif{r}}{r^2} \\
        &= \lambda k \hat{r} (\frac{-1}{a+L} + \frac{1}{a}) \\
        &= \lambda k (\hat{\imath}) (\frac{L}{a(a+L)})\\
        &= \boxed{\frac{Qk\hat{\imath}}{a(a+L)}}
    \end{align*}
    Note when $a >> L$, it degenerates the the below, which suggests that it acts as a point charge at sufficently large distances.
    \[\vec{E} \approx \frac{Qk\hat{\imath}}{a^2}\]
\end{answer}

\begin{example}
    Determine the electric field at P.
    \begin{center}
        \includegraphics[scale=0.5]{ex_ring_e.jpg}
    \end{center}
\end{example}
\begin{answer}
    Note that "horizontal" $\odif{\vec{E}} \sin{\theta}$ components cancel via symmetry. Thus, you only need to consider components proportional to $\cos{\theta}$.
    \begin{align*}
        \vec{E} &= k \int \frac{\odif{Q}}{a^2 + x^2} \hat{\imath} \cos{\theta} \\
        &= k \hat{\imath} \int \frac{\odif{Q}}{a^2 + x^2} \times \frac{x}{\sqrt{a^2 + x^2}} \\
        &= \boxed{\frac{k \hat{\imath} Qx}{(a^2 + x^2) ^ {1.5}}}
    \end{align*}
\end{answer}

\subsection{Electric Flux}
Flux describes the amount of a given quantity that passes through a given area.
\textbf{Electric flux} $\Phi_E$ describes the amount of electric field that passes through a defined area. Note the area vector is defined to be outward from a closed surface.

\[\Phi_E = \vec{E} \cdot \vec{A} \]

For an arbitrary surface:

\[\Phi_E = \int \vec{E} \cdot \odif{\vec{A}}\]

\subsection{Gauss's Law}
\begin{definition}[Gauss's Law]
    For an arbitrary \textbf{Gaussian Surface} (3d closed surface):
    \[\Phi_E = \oint \vec{E} \cdot \odif{\vec{A}} = \frac{q_{enc}}{\epsilon_0}\]
    Usually you can rig the surface such that the electric field is perpendicular or parallel to all components of the area, with a sphere or a cylinder.
\end{definition}
\begin{example}
    Determine the electric field surrounding an infinitely large, thin plane of positive changes with uniform surface charge density.
    \begin{center}
        \includegraphics[scale=0.5]{ex_gauss.png}
    \end{center}
\end{example}
\begin{answer}
    Set up the Gaussian surface as shown.
    The area vector is perpendicular to the electric field along the "wrapping" of the cylinder.
    The area vector is parallel to the electric field in the same direction for the two "caps" of the cylinder.
    Thus,
    \[2EA = \frac{q_{enc}}{\epsilon_0} = \frac{\sigma A}{\epsilon_0} \implies E = \boxed{\frac{\sigma}{2 \epsilon_0}}\]

    Note for the case of a two "infinite" plane capacitor, we assume the field outside the capacitor cancels. Thus,
    \[E_{capacitor} = \frac{\sigma}{\epsilon_0}\]
\end{answer}

\subsection{Electric Potential Energy}
EPE of a system = amount of work required by external force to bring point charges to current positions from infinitely far away.
Total EPE = sum of EPE between each pair of charged particles.
\[U_E = k \frac{q_1q_2}{r}\]

\begin{proof}[Proof of EPE formula]
    Bringing $q_1$ to $q_2$ from infinity is equivalent to bringing $q_2$ to $q_1$ from infinity.
    EPE gained by work done to bring one charge to the given distance away.
    \begin{align*}
        \textrm{EPE} = W &= -\int \vec{F} \cdot \odif{\vec{r}} \\
        &= -\int_{-\infty}^{r} k \frac{q_1 q_2}{x^2} \odif{x} \\
        &= -k q_1q_2 (- \frac{1}{r} - \frac{1}{\infty}) \\
        &= \boxed{k\frac{q_1 q_2}{r}} 
    \end{align*}
\end{proof}
\subsection{Electric Potential}
Electric potential describes the \textit{potential energy} per unit charge at a point in space. Note it is a scalar.
The electric force is \textbf{conservative}: change in EPE due to position change does not depend on the path.
Electric potentials caused by different charges add.

\[V = k \frac{q}{r} \Leftrightarrow V = k \int \frac{\odif{q}}{r} \]

The electric potential difference between two points is the change in EPE between the two points:
\[\Delta V = \frac{\Delta U_e}{q} = -\int \vec{E} \cdot \odif{\vec{r}} = -Ed\]

for perpendicular distance d. The integral form comes from the work form of EPE divided by q.

Note the integral form suggests the following:

\[\vec{E}_x = -\odv{V}{x}\]

Also note the definition of potential suggests positive charges want to go to low electric potential (loses EPE => KE)

\subsection{Conservation of Electric Energy}

EPE is a type of energy, so it is conserved by the law of energy conservation.
